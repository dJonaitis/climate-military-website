The Gap in Data

The reporting on just how much this massive military industrial complex emits is extremely lackluster. This make analysing who the biggest military emitters are extremely difficult to answer.

The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) has 198 parties, and is ratified by 50 states (as of 2024). The data below comes from them, compiled by The Military Emissions Gap.


Reporting military emissions is voluntary.

When the world's leaders gather to discuss how to address climate change and tackle the existential threat that it is, one institution remains absent from those discussions: the military. The Kyoto Protocol, in 1997, under the behest of the United States was designed to exempt military emissions from the carbon reduction goals set out. The 2015 Paris Agreement ceremoniously removed the exemption for military emissions and instead made it voluntary.

In 2015, at COP21 in Paris, the leaders of the world met again and proclaimed: We've done it! The Paris Agreement was signed. Military emissions are no longer exempt, they said. While the Paris Agreement overturned the exemption, it also made reporting military emissions voluntary, citing concerns over national security. And so it remains today, no country has obligations to report their military emissions.

Even if military emissions were mandatory to report, the UNFCCC has different reporting obligations for countries at different levels of development. Thus, even if the leaders had said: There must be more accountability, military emission reporting is now obligatory, only 43 countries would have to report them. These "more developed" nations are known as Annex I countries. This designation excludes large players like China or Israel.

What data do we have?

That is no less grim.

36 countries have their military emissions data accessibility classified as poor or worse. Only Cyprus, Germany, Hungary and Slovakia have their data accessibility rated as fair.
